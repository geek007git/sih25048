1913

Bohr's Model

Niels Bohr proposed that electrons orbit the nucleus in specific energy levels or shells, and can only gain or lose energy by jumping between these discrete levels.

1920s

Quantum Mechanical Model

Development of quantum mechanics led to our modern understanding of electrons as existing in probability clouds called orbitals rather than in defined orbits.

Electron Configuration

Electrons are arranged in energy levels or shells around the nucleus. Each shell can hold a specific maximum number of electrons, and the arrangement of electrons determines an element's chemical properties.

Isotopes and Ions

Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons) but with different numbers of neutrons. This results in atoms with the same atomic number but different mass numbers.

Example: Carbon-12, Carbon-13, and Carbon-14 are all isotopes of carbon with 6 protons but 6, 7, and 8 neutrons respectively.

Ions

Ions are atoms that have gained or lost electrons, resulting in a net positive or negative charge. When atoms lose electrons, they form positive ions (cations). When they gain electrons, they form negative ions (anions).

What is an Atom?

An atom is the smallest unit of ordinary matter that forms a chemical element. Every solid, liquid, gas, and plasma is composed of neutral or ionized atoms. Atoms are extremely small, typically around 100 picometers across. They are made up of three subatomic particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons.

Subatomic Particles

Atoms consist of three primary subatomic particles, each with distinct properties that determine how atoms behave and interact.

Protons

Positively charged particles found in the nucleus. The number of protons defines the element (atomic number). Each proton has a relative charge of +1 and a mass of approximately 1 atomic mass unit (amu).

Neutrons

Neutral particles (no charge) found in the nucleus alongside protons. They contribute to the atom's mass but not its charge. Different numbers of neutrons create isotopes of the same element.

Electrons

Negatively charged particles that orbit the nucleus in electron shells or energy levels. Each electron has a relative charge of -1 and a mass that is approximately 1/1836 that of a proton.

Atomic Number (Z) = Number of Protons
Mass Number (A) = Number of Protons + Number of Neutrons
For a neutral atom: Number of Protons = Number of Electrons
Isotopes have the same atomic number (Z) but different mass numbers (A)

Evolution of Atomic Theory

~440 BCE

Democritus

Ancient Greek philosopher who first proposed that all matter consists of indivisible particles called "atomos" (meaning "uncuttable" in Greek).

1803

Dalton's Atomic Theory

John Dalton proposed that all matter is made of atoms, which are indivisible and indestructible. Each element has its own unique type of atom.

1897

J.J. Thomson's Plum Pudding Model

After discovering the electron, Thomson proposed that atoms were a positively charged "pudding" with negatively charged electrons embedded throughout.

1911

Rutherford's Nuclear Model

Ernest Rutherford's gold foil experiment revealed that most of an atom's mass is concentrated in a tiny, positively charged nucleus with electrons orbiting around it.
